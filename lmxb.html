Investigating Detection Biases in the Black Hole X-ray Binary Population

Sponsored by NSF CIERA REU under the supervision of Vicky Kalogera and Christopher Berry


What are black hole X-ray binaries?

Black hole X-ray binaries are composed of a black hole accreting mass from a non-degenerate star —either through Roche lobe overflow (RLO) or via captured winds from a massive star. Currently, there are twenty confirmed X-ray binary systems of which fifteen are RLO-fed and five are wind-fed.

For a review of black hole X-ray binaries see Remillard et al. 2006 and for a general review of gravitational wave progenitors see Mandel et al. 2018.


What can they tell us?

Black hole X-ray binary systems present a remarkable opportunity to not only investigate the physics governing black holes, but also a slew of mechanisms responsible for black hole formation, including supernova physics, the distribution of massive stars, and the division between compact objects (e.g. white dwarfs, neutron stars, and black holes).

We are particularly interested in transient black hole X-ray binaries, because these systems are amenable to mass estimation. Thus far, accurate mass estimation for black hole X-ray binaries requires use of radial velocity measurements from the donor star. In order to avoid contamination of the donor spectra, this method requires the system to cycle through periods of dim quiescence. Due to this requirement, the current targets for mass estimation are black hole X-ray binaries undergoing RLO. These systems are prefered because the mass transfer from RLO forms an accretion disk around the black hole, and due to instabilities within the disk, the system will undergo cycles of bright outburst and dim quiescence.


What are we studying?

Using the population of observed RLO black hole X-ray binaries, prior studies have attempted to constrain the underlying stellar-mass black hole distribution. Contrary to theoretical studies, each of these investigations identified a 'gap' between the lowest mass black holes and the maximum neutron star mass. The two prevailing explanations for this discrepancy are either modifications in the theoretical treatment of supernova energetics or a selection bias that suppresses the detection of low-mass black holes. We are investigating this second proposal: potential detection biases in the search for black hole X-ray binary systems. In order to conduct our analysis, we first synthesized a population of binary systems representative of the Milky way. For our target population of black hole X-ray binary systems, we selected all sources composed of a black hole accreting mass from a donor star via RLO. We then determined whether each source was transient or persistent via the disk instability model.

In order to investigate the detectability of the transient systems, we applied a Monte Carlo artificial observing scheme. For each transient source, we first produce a light curve according to the disk instability model. Each light curve is then run through a toy observation campaign using N random starts. The effective detection probability is then the number of campaigns that observed both outburst and quiescence. See the attached figure for a diagram of this process.

For a review of the disk instability model see Lasota et al. 2016.
